## Why Foot Care Matters for Diabetic Patients

Diabetes can cause two major problems that put your feet at serious risk: peripheral neuropathy and poor blood circulation. Peripheral neuropathy damages the nerves in your feet, reducing your ability to feel pain, heat, or cold. This means you may not notice a cut, blister, or sore until it becomes infected. Poor circulation slows down healing, making even minor wounds potentially dangerous. According to the International Diabetes Federation, diabetic foot complications are the leading cause of non-traumatic lower limb amputations worldwide. The good news is that up to 85% of diabetes-related amputations are preventable with proper foot care and early intervention.

## Daily Foot Inspection and Hygiene

Every diabetic patient should inspect their feet thoroughly each day. Look at the tops, bottoms, sides, heels, and between the toes for any cuts, blisters, redness, swelling, calluses, or changes in skin colour. If you have difficulty seeing the bottom of your feet, use a mirror or ask a family member to help. Wash your feet daily with lukewarm water and mild soap, but avoid soaking them for extended periods as this can dry out the skin. After washing, dry your feet gently and completely, paying special attention to the spaces between your toes where moisture can lead to fungal infections. Apply a fragrance-free moisturising lotion to the tops and bottoms of your feet, but avoid applying it between the toes.

## Choosing the Right Footwear

Proper footwear is one of the most important aspects of diabetic foot care. Never walk barefoot, even indoors, as you may step on something sharp without feeling it. Choose shoes that fit well with a wide toe box, cushioned soles, and no internal seams that could cause friction. Always check inside your shoes before putting them on to make sure there are no pebbles, rough spots, or foreign objects. Wear clean, dry socks made of moisture-wicking materials and avoid socks with tight elastic bands that restrict circulation. ## Moisture Control and Nail Care

Keeping your feet at the right moisture level is essential. Skin that is too dry can crack, creating entry points for bacteria, while skin that is too moist (especially between the toes) can encourage fungal growth. Trim your toenails straight across and file the edges gently with an emery board to avoid ingrown toenails. Never cut your cuticles or use sharp objects to clean around your nails. If your nails are thick, discoloured, or difficult to trim, have them cared for by a healthcare professional. Avoid using over-the-counter corn or callus removers, as the chemicals in these products can damage diabetic skin. Instead, have corns and calluses treated professionally.

## When to See a Doctor: Warning Signs

Seek medical attention promptly if you notice any of the following: a cut or wound that is not healing within a few days, redness or warmth spreading around a wound, pus or foul-smelling drainage from any foot sore, blackened or discoloured skin, persistent pain or tingling, or any change in the shape of your foot.
